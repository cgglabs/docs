Motors: KT & KV

April 1, 2025 · 4 min read
Carlos Gonzalez
Chief Hacker @ CGG Labs

If you pick up the data sheet for a motor, one of the first things you'll see are the KVK_VKV​ and KTK_TKT​ ratings.

A lot of motors even have these values written on the rotor.

But what do they mean?

1. The Relationship Between Torque & Current​

Electric motors convert electrical energy into mechanical energy, with torque being the force that causes rotation.

In a simplified DC motor model, the generated torque TTT is directly proportional to the armature current III:

T=KT⋅IT = K_T \cdot IT=KT​⋅I

where:

  • TTT is the torque (in Newton-meters, N·m),
  • III is the current (in Amperes, A),
  • KTK_TKT​ is the torque constant (in N·m/A).

This equation indicates that for every ampere of current, the motor produces a proportional amount of torque as determined by KTK_TKT​.

2. The Relationship Between Speed & Voltage​

The motor’s speed—typically measured as the angular velocity ω\omegaω (in radians per second) or in revolutions per minute (rpm)—is primarily governed by the voltage applied to the motor. As the motor spins, it generates a back electromotive force (back EMF) that is proportional to its speed:

V=KE⋅ωV = K_E \cdot \omegaV=KE​⋅ω

where:

  • VVV is the applied voltage (in Volts, V),
  • ω\omegaω is the angular velocity (in rad/s),
  • KEK_EKE​ is the back EMF constant (in V/(rad/s)).

Under no-load conditions, the back EMF nearly equals the applied voltage, linking voltage directly to speed.

3. Introducing KVK_VKV​ and KTK_TKT​​

KVK_VKV​: The Motor Velocity Constant​

  • Definition: KVK_VKV​ is typically expressed as the number of revolutions per minute (rpm) the motor achieves per volt under no load.
  • Units: rpm/V.
  • Interpretation: For instance, if a motor has a KVK_VKV​ of 1000 rpm/V, applying 1 volt ideally produces 1000 rpm, and 10 volts yield 10,000 rpm.

KTK_TKT​: The Motor Torque Constant​

  • Definition: KTK_TKT​ represents the torque produced per ampere of current.
  • Units: N·m/A.
  • Interpretation: A motor with a KTK_TKT​ of 0.05 N·m/A will generate 0.05 N·m of torque for every 1 ampere of current.

In ideal motors using SI units, the back EMF constant KEK_EKE​ and the torque constant KTK_TKT​ are numerically equal, reflecting energy conservation within the motor.

4. Deriving the Relationship Between KTK_TKT​ and KVK_VKV​​

4.1 Converting KVK_VKV​ to SI-Compatible Units​

KVK_VKV​ is typically given in rpm/V, but for consistency with SI units (where angular velocity is in rad/s), we need to convert:

ω=rpm×2π60\omega = \text{rpm} \times \frac{2\pi}{60}ω=rpm×602π​

If the motor’s speed per volt is KVK_VKV​ rpm/V, then in SI units the speed per volt becomes:

KV′=KV×2π60K_V^{\prime} = K_V \times \frac{2\pi}{60}KV′​=KV​×602π​

4.2 Relating Back EMF to Motor Speed​

The back EMF EEE generated in the motor is given by:

E=KE⋅ωE = K_E \cdot \omegaE=KE​⋅ω

Under no-load conditions, the applied voltage VVV almost equals the back EMF:

V≈KE⋅ωV \approx K_E \cdot \omegaV≈KE​⋅ω

Thus, for a given voltage, the speed is approximately:

ω≈VKE\omega \approx \frac{V}{K_E}ω≈KE​V​

Considering the SI conversion, where the no-load speed per volt is KV′K_V′KV​′:

VKE=KV′⋅V\frac{V}{K_E} = K_V^{\prime} \cdot VKE​V​=KV′​⋅V

By canceling VVV (assuming V≠0V \neq 0V=0), we obtain:

KE=1KV′K_E = \frac{1}{K_V^{\prime}}KE​=KV′​1​

Replacing KV′K_V′KV​′ with the converted form gives:

KE=1KV×2π60=602πKVK_E = \frac{1}{K_V \times \frac{2\pi}{60}} = \frac{60}{2\pi K_V}KE​=KV​×602π​1​=2πKV​60​

4.3 Establishing the Relationship with KTK_TKT​​

For an ideal motor, energy conservation dictates that the electrical power equals the mechanical power (ignoring losses):

I⋅V=T⋅ωI \cdot V = T \cdot \omegaI⋅V=T⋅ω

Substitute the expressions T=KT⋅IT = K_T \cdot IT=KT​⋅I and V=KE⋅ωV = K_E \cdot \omegaV=KE​⋅ω:

I⋅(KE⋅ω)=(KT⋅I)⋅ωI \cdot (K_E \cdot \omega) = (K_T \cdot I) \cdot \omegaI⋅(KE​⋅ω)=(KT​⋅I)⋅ω

Canceling III and ω\omegaω (assuming they are non-zero):

KE=KTK_E = K_TKE​=KT​

Given the earlier result for KEK_EKE​, it follows that:

KT=602πKVK_T = \frac{60}{2\pi K_V}KT​=2πKV​60​

This equation shows the direct mathematical relationship between the torque constant KTK_TKT​ (in N·m/A) and the velocity constant KVK_VKV​ (in rpm/V).

5. Okay... So what?​

All the equations above are saying is that there's a tradeoff between KTK_TKT​ and KVK_VKV​.

And since KTK_TKT​ is proportional to torque and KVK_VKV​ is proportional to speed, then there is a fundamental tradeoff between torque and speed. We can't just increase or decrease both.

KT=602πKVK_T = \frac{60}{2\pi K_V}KT​=2πKV​60​

Understanding these relationships is crucial for selecting and analyzing motors in applications ranging from drones and robotics to industrial machinery. It provides insight into how electrical inputs are converted into mechanical outputs, enabling optimal design and control of motor-driven systems.
